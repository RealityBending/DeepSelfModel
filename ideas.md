The Active Inference Personality Framework
-	Alternative names: Core Prior Cascade (CPC) Model of Self/Personality 
Overview: The Active Inference Personality Framework is a generative, hierarchical model of individual differences grounded in cybernetics, predictive processing, and the Free Energy Principle. Unlike descriptive psychometric models (e.g., the Big Five) that cluster observed behaviors, this framework posits that personality states and traits are the downstream consequences of deeply entrenched hyperpriors - the fundamental, largely unconscious beliefs a living agent holds about the nature of reality, the self, and the solvability of prediction errors.
In this view, the human organism is a predictive engine striving to minimize expected free energy. Personality is not a static set of labels, but a characteristic cybernetic strategy. At the apex of the hierarchy are "existential hyperpriors" governing temporal horizons and environmental volatility. These global parameters cascade downward, constraining how the organism manages its structural boundaries (its Markov blanket) and how it updates its internal models. Therefore, what we observe as a "personality trait" (like extroversion or neuroticism) is actually a highly weighted, self-fulfilling epistemic policy utilized by the agent to survive irreducible uncertainty. 
The framework positions itself as a generative account of the mechanisms underlying descriptive phenotypes identified by existing models (e.g., the Big Five, BIS/BAS, Attachment Theory), rather than as a replacement of them. What those frameworks describe behaviorally, this framework proposes to explain mechanistically.

1)	Existential Hyperpriors (Global Trajectory & Volatility)
These sit at the absolute apex. They are the deepest assumptions the agent holds about the nature of reality, time, and the fundamental solvability of prediction errors.
•	Volatility Prior (Environmental Variance): The agent's baseline expectation regarding the rate of change and stability of the environment's hidden states.
o	High Volatility (Chaotic): The expectation that the rules governing the environment shift rapidly. Forces the system to discount past learning and expect constant prediction errors.
o	Low Volatility (Static): The expectation that the environment is highly predictable and structurally stable over time.
•	Tractability Prior (Action Efficacy): The agent's baseline expectation of whether prediction errors, once encountered, can actually be resolved through the agent's own active inference.
o	High Tractability: Belief that action resolves error (Agency). Leads to approach behavior and behavioral activation, even in highly volatile environments.
o	Low Tractability: Belief that action generates unresolvable error, or that the system lacks the capacity to shift external states (Helplessness). Leads to epistemic shielding and behavioral inhibition.
•	Irreducible Uncertainty Tolerance: How the system handles ambiguity when no epistemic foraging can resolve the prediction error. This is a reactive parameter governing the agent's response when an error proves unresolvable after the fact. It is formally distinct from the tractability prior and can dissociate: an agent with high tractability can be brittle on encountering genuinely unresolvable errors; an agent with chronically low tractability can develop high tolerance for residual uncertainty as a baseline condition.)
o	High Tolerance: Acceptance of unresolvable error; epistemic humility; maintaining functioning without forcing premature model updates.
o	Low Tolerance: Premature cognitive closure; distress in ambiguity; forcing lower-level models to fit incomplete data.
•	Affective Valence (Hedonic Baseline): The characteristic phenomenological tone of the agent's expected free energy signal. In FEP terms, persistent negative affect is the felt correlate of chronically elevated expected free energy — the agent continuously anticipates unresolvable prediction errors, experienced as anxiety, dysphoria, or anhedonia. This dimension is the phenomenological downstream consequence of the Tractability Prior and is made explicit here because it carries independent predictive value and maps directly onto the construct of Neuroticism in descriptive models.
o	Positive Valence: Low baseline expected free energy; affective tone of approach, safety, and reward anticipation.
o	Negative Valence: High baseline expected free energy; affective tone of chronic threat anticipation, vigilance, or hedonic blunting.
•	Terminal Prior (Temporal Horizon of the Self): How the agent models its ultimate structural dissolution. How far into the future the agent projects its self-model before anticipating structural dissolution (death). Mortality salience forces the agent to resolve the ultimate prediction error of self-model cessation.
o	Disengagement: Attenuation of the temporal self-model, taking two distinct forms:
	Passive Disengagement: Diffuse failure of deep temporal planning; anhedonic or fatalistic orientation; the agent drifts rather than actively converging on a terminal state. Characterized by generalized low precision on future-directed inference.
	Active Cessation-Seeking: A catastrophically precise prior converging on systemic shutdown as the optimal policy. Expected free energy of all future states is evaluated as unresolvably high; systemic cessation is actively preferred as the lowest-free-energy attractor. This is the formal analog of Thanatos and maps onto clinical suicidal ideation at the extreme.
o	Integration: Authentic acceptance of finitude; expected free energy is balanced without needing to outrun death. Focus on present-horizon optimization.
o	Transcendence: Extending the Markov blanket across time via legacy (cultural, symbolic, or biological/familial). The drive to make the "Self" model outlive the physical substrate.
Note that Transcendence and Integration may represent two functional responses along a single dimension — degree of temporal self-extension — while Disengagement represents a qualitatively distinct attractor state. Finitude management is conceptually mapped onto Terror Management Theory (Greenberg, Pyszczynski & Solomon, 1986) and Becker's Denial of Death (1973): mortality salience functions here as a hyperprior precision-weighting problem, where confrontation with finitude forces the agent to resolve or suppress the prediction error of self-model dissolution.
2)	 Boundary & Relational Priors (The Markov Blanket)
These govern the expected state of the boundary between the internal model and the external world.
•	Permeability Prior (Agency vs. Communion): The relative precision weighting the agent assigns to self-referential priors versus exteroceptive/social signals. Formally, this is not the Markov blanket becoming "permeable" — a blanket that dissolves takes the subject with it — but rather an adjustment of the gradient of influence across the blanket: how strongly external states are permitted to update internal ones, and vice versa. High coupling (Dissolution Drive) reflects elevated precision on exteroceptive/social signals relative to self-referential priors; low coupling (Differentiation Drive) reflects the reverse. This maps onto Bakan's (1966) agency-communion dimension and onto secure, avoidant, and anxiously-preoccupied attachment styles. Ontological goal: to tend toward merging, affiliating, and distributing cognitive load — or toward differentiating, asserting, and maintaining internal sovereignty?
o	Differentiation Drive: The expectation that internal states must remain distinct from external states (Low Coupling, Rigid Blanket). High drive for autonomy, status, and individuation. The extreme differentiation results in desire for power and dominance: it minimizes error by forcing the external social environment to synchronize with the agent's internal model. It is active, outward-facing error minimization through environmental subjugation.
o	Dissolution Drive: The expectation that internal states should synchronize with external states (High Coupling, Permeable Blanket). High drive for affiliation, merging, and collective regulation.
•	Social Inference Prior (Mentalizing): The precision assigned to modeling the hidden states of other agents.
o	High Social Precision: Deeply invested in simulating others' internal models (empathy, hyper-vigilance to social cues).
o	Low Social Precision: Treating other agents more like physical environmental variables than nested cognitive systems.
•	Sensory Anchoring Prior (Blanket Thickness): The relative precision weighting assigned to the foundational sensory channels the system utilizes as its ultimate "ground truth" to construct the minimal self and resolve higher-tier prediction errors. This defines the informational thickness of the Markov blanket.
o	Exteroceptive Anchoring: Baseline trust directed outward. The system prioritizes environmental states (visual, auditory, spatial) to validate its internal models. The self is defined in relation to external spatial coordinates and exteroceptive feedback.
o	Interoceptive Anchoring: Baseline trust directed inward. The continuous monitoring of cardiac, respiratory, and visceral signals forms the bedrock of reality-testing for the self-model. The minimal self is tightly bound to internal homeostatic and physiological rhythms.
3)	Epistemic Meta-Priors (Error Resolution & Updating)
These govern how the agent prefers to minimize free energy and update its models based on the constraints set by Tiers 1 and 2.
•	Precision Bias: 
o	 Prior-Heavy (Top-Down): High precision on internal beliefs. The agent minimizes error by altering the world to fit expectations or ignoring contradictory data (Assimilation/Stability).
o	Sensory-Heavy (Bottom-Up): High precision on incoming errors. The agent minimizes error by rapidly rewriting its internal models (Accommodation/Plasticity).
•	Action-Perception Precision: The agent cannot explicitly "choose" between perceptual and active inference; both occur continuously. Instead, this dimension captures the relative precision the agent assigns to its action models (motor outputs) versus its perceptual updates (belief revision) when faced with free energy.
o	Action-Heavy (World-Transformation): High precision is placed on the agent's ability to alter external states. The agent minimizes free energy by exerting control, manipulating the environment, or forcing external reality to conform to its internal model (Active Inference dominance). Maps onto external locus of control and behavioral dominance.
o	Perception-Heavy (Self-Transformation): Low precision is placed on the efficacy of action models relative to sensory updating. The agent minimizes free energy by revising its own internal beliefs, shifting selective attention, or accommodating the environment (Perceptual Inference dominance). Maps onto internal locus of control, accommodation, and occasionally behavioral inhibition.
•	Epistemic Foraging Drive: The expected information gain of sampling high-variance states.
o	High Foraging: Actively seeking boundary-stressing environments (somatic or semantic) because the agent expects this will ultimately reduce long-term uncertainty and prevent overfitting. (Exploration/Sensation-seeking).
o	Low Foraging: Preference for highly predictable, low-variance environments.
o	Somatic (Biological) Foraging: Testing the physical Markov blanket. The agent injects prediction errors into the physiological system to unequivocally prove the existence and resilience of the minimal, bodily self. On the mild end, this translates into common natural perturbations of sensing via exercise, breathwork, or interoceptive focus. On the extreme end, it is a form of biological edge-seeking (e.g., via BDSM, extreme sports), where visceral threat is used to unequivocally prove the existence of the minimal, bodily self.
o	Semantic (Phenomenological) Foraging: Testing the conceptual and cultural models. The agent injects prediction errors into their psychological architecture to see if their worldview holds up. On the mild end, this is engaging with transgressive art, complex esoteric symbolic systems, or radical philosophy to see if one's worldview holds up. On the extreme end, it is a form of phenomenological edge-seeking (psychedelics, altered sates of consciousness, psychotic states), deliberately shattering the conceptual ego to observe its underlying structure.
•	Temporal Depth: The functional time horizon for active inference.
o	Shallow: Optimizing for immediate sensory feedback and homeostasis (reactive).
o	Deep: Simulating cascading future states and optimizing for allostasis (proactive).
•	Self-Model Coherence: The temporal stability and narrative integration of the self-model. In FEP terms, this is the precision and temporal consistency of the highest-level self-referential priors. 
o	Low coherence: where the self-model is revised rapidly and discontinuously in response to local prediction errors. Low Coherence leads to fragmented or rapidly-revised self-model; identity discontinuity; vulnerability to self-model dissolution under social or somatic stress (identity diffusion, emotional dysregulation, and borderline-spectrum presentations). 
o	High coherence: Maps onto identity consolidation and narrative continuity (McAdams, Erikson/Marcia). High Coherence leads to stable, temporally integrated self-model resistant to local perturbation.

Here is a hypothesized factor structure:
Hypothesized Factor	Primary Construct	Highly Correlated Dimensions (The "Syndrome")
Factor 1: Epistemic Plasticity	How the system handles new information.	Sensory-Heavy Precision Bias, High Epistemic Foraging, High Uncertainty Tolerance.
Factor 2: Cybernetic Stability	The baseline expectation of safety vs. threat.	High Tractability Prior, Positive Affective Valence, Low Volatility Prior. (Negative correlate: Epistemic Shielding).
Factor 3: Blanket Permeability	How the self relates to the group.	High Dissolution Drive, High Social Inference Precision.
Factor 4: Temporal Extension	The timescale of the agent's generative model.	Deep Temporal Depth, Terminal Transcendence, High Differentiation Drive (building a legacy requires asserting the self).
Factor 5: Self-Model Stability	The temporal integration and coherence of the self-model.	High Self-Model Coherence, High Tractability Prior, Deep Temporal Depth. (Negative correlate: Low Uncertainty Tolerance, Active Cessation-Seeking Terminal pole.)
