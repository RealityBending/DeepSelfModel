The model organizes personality as a hierarchical generative framework, progressing from deep computational assumptions about the world down to observable behavioral traits. It is structured across four distinct levels:

Level 1: Ultra-Priors
These are the foundational computational parameters of the generative model, representing the agent's baseline expectations about reality.

Volatility: The baseline expectation about how quickly the environment's hidden rules change. High volatility discounts past learning, increases the learning rate, and keeps the system braced for fresh prediction errors.

Tractability: The expectation that action can reliably reduce prediction error. High tractability supports agency, approach, and policy execution, while low tractability pushes the system toward helplessness and behavioral inhibition.

Noise: The expectation about irreducible aleatoric uncertainty—how much ambient ambiguity must simply be tolerated without triggering drastic internal model updates.

Terminal: The temporal horizon the self can sustain before anticipating dissolution. This dictates the system's integration of finitude and attempts to extend the self through legacy or transcendence.

Level 2: Affective Priors
These nodes act as the deep, felt readouts generated downstream of the Ultra-Priors.

Valence: The affective readout of baseline expected free energy. It serves as the felt sense of safety versus chronic threat.

Uncertainty: The expected opacity of the world in the moment. It translates environmental volatility and noise into how much ambient ambiguity or confusion the system expects to experience.

Temporal Efficacy: The felt depth of future planning and efficacy across time. It dictates how well long-horizon control can be sustained.

Level 3: Precision Biases
These are operational biases that govern how the system allocates precision (attention and confidence) to manage the affects generated at Level 2.

Sensory Anchoring: A bias dictating where reality is grounded. It determines whether the self is stabilized primarily by internal bodily signals (interoception) or external sensory structure (exteroception).

Coupling Bias: The bias governing how tightly the self should couple to other people and the wider environment, balancing differentiation/autonomy against synchrony/affiliation.

Action Perception: The relative precision placed on changing the world (active control) versus changing internal beliefs (perceptual accommodation).

Epistemic Foraging: The expected value of sampling uncertainty, functioning as the core epistemic-gain drive behind exploration and curiosity.

Narrative Precision: The precision assigned to autobiographical continuity and the narrative self. Higher values stabilize identity across contexts, while lower values allow fluid updating at the risk of fragmentation.

Level 4: Observables
These are the final stable behavioral attractors generated by the upstream cascade, mapping onto established personality traits.

Neuroticism: A behavioral attractor generated by chronically elevated valence expectations (threat) and interoceptive anchoring, characterized by hyper-vigilance to internal arousal.

Extraversion: An outward-oriented attractor driven by high environmental coupling and a bias toward action over perception, manifesting as reward-seeking and social synchrony.

Openness: Driven by high epistemic-gain expectations and highly fluid narrative precision, manifesting as exploratory edge-testing and a willingness to revise the self-model.

Agreeableness: Rooted in strong coupling biases and feeling secure in the environment (low threat), resulting in an orientation toward affiliation, trust, and structural alignment with others.

Conscientiousness: Rooted in deep temporal efficacy, rigid self-coherence, and a bias toward action, experienced as an organized, persistent exertion of cybernetic control over the environment.
