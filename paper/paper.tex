\abstract{Inter-individual differences in personality currently suffer
from a weak generative and mechanistic conceptualisation. By bridging
active inference frameworks with established personality accounts, we
introduce a principled hierarchical predictive architecture grounded in
the Free Energy Principle. In contrast to descriptive psychometric
frameworks that map behavioural phenotypes, the Deep-Self Predictive
Cascade Model proposes that observable personality traits are the
downstream expressions of deep prior expectations and precision biases
that shape how an agent interprets uncertainty, regulates action, and
maintains a structural sense of self. Within this architecture, traits
are conceptualised not as static endowments, but as stable attractor
basins within a Free Energy landscape. The hierarchy cascades from
foundational ultra-priors governing epistemic, allostatic, and
teleological expectations, through physiological affective states, and
into the precision-weighting of the minimal, agentic, and narrative
self-models. By parameterising these computational layers, the Deep-Self
Model naturally generates the phenotypic profiles described by the Big
Five, Cybernetic Big Five Theory, BIS/BAS, and the HiTOP dimensional
taxonomy of psychopathology. This framework offers a unified
computational ontology for personality, yielding testable predictions
for cognitive neuroscience, psychophysiology, and computational
psychiatry.  \section{Impact Statement} \noindent The Deep-Self
Predictive Cascade Model provides a mechanistic, mathematically grounded
account of how hierarchical expectations and precision biases generate
the full range of observable personality phenotypes, offering a
principled bridge between the Free Energy Principle and the scientific
study of individual differences. }

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Personality psychology currently faces a persistent explanatory gap.
Descriptive frameworks, most notably the Five-Factor Model
(\citeproc{ref-goldberg1990alternative}{Goldberg, 1990};
\citeproc{ref-mccrae1992introduction}{McCrae \& John, 1992}), have
proven remarkably successful at organising individual differences in
behaviour, affect, and cognition. However, their structural parsimony
comes at a theoretical cost. These models describe phenotypic variance
across populations but offer limited mechanistic accounts of the
generative processes that produce and maintain such variation within the
individual.

Several frameworks have attempted to bridge this descriptive-process
divide. The Cognitive-Affective Personality System (CAPS,
\citeproc{ref-mischel1995cognitive}{Mischel \& Shoda, 1995})
conceptualises personality as a network of interacting cognitive and
affective units, while Cybernetic Big Five Theory (CB5T,
\citeproc{ref-deyoung2015cybernetic}{DeYoung, 2015}) offers a
functionalist account of traits as the typical parameters of a
cybernetic system optimising for distinct goals. Yet, while these models
successfully introduce process dynamics, they lack a unified
mathematical ontology to explain \emph{why} certain cognitive-affective
parameters reliably coalesce into stable phenotypes.

The Free Energy Principle (FEP, \citeproc{ref-friston2010free}{Friston,
2010}) and its corollary process theory, active inference, offer a
principled candidate for such an ontology. Under the FEP,
self-organising biological systems maintain their structural integrity
by minimising the upper bound on their own surprise---formally defined
as variational free energy. This is achieved by continuously updating
probabilistic generative models of their environment via perceptual
inference (revising internal models) and active inference (acting upon
the world).

Applied to individual differences, this implies that personality
characteristics are not fixed endowments. Rather, they are the
downstream behavioural and affective consequences of deeply entrenched
probabilistic parameters. To formalise this, we introduce the
\textbf{Deep-Self Predictive Cascade Model}. This model posits that
personality is fundamentally a \emph{Free Energy Landscape}---a
topographic surface mapping achievable parameter configurations against
their associated expected free energy.

The binary distinction between expectations and biases clarifies this
topology: \textbf{Expectations (location parameters, \(\mu\))} determine
\emph{where} the attractor basins are carved into the landscape,
establishing the underlying assumptions the agent holds about reality.
\textbf{Biases (precision weightings, \(\pi\))} determine \emph{how deep
and steep} those basins are, defining how easily the system can be
dislodged from its habitual states by sensory noise. In this view,
traits are not things we have; they are the attractors we reliably fall
into.

\section{The Deep-Self Predictive Cascade
Model}\label{the-deep-self-predictive-cascade-model}

The Deep-Self Model is structured as a hierarchical cascade comprising
four distinct computational levels. Information propagates downstream,
wherein the precision of higher-level prior expectations acts as a
multiplier on their downward influence, structurally dictating the
parameters of the lower levels.

\subsection{Level 1: Ultra-Priors}\label{level-1-ultra-priors}

At the apex of the hierarchy are ``Ultra-Priors''---the system's
unconditioned, foundational expectations regarding the structure of
reality and the agent's capacity to navigate it. These occupy the
absolute apex of the cascade, acting as hyper-parameters unconstrained
by higher psychological structures.

\subsubsection{The Epistemic Imperative}\label{the-epistemic-imperative}

This imperative drives the resolution and management of uncertainty,
dictating how the generative model handles world-opacity. *
\textbf{Volatility:} The baseline expectation regarding the rate of
environmental change (the \(\omega\) parameter in Hierarchical Gaussian
Filtering; Mathys (\citeproc{ref-mathys2011filter}{2011})). High
expected volatility heavily discounts past learning and braces the
system for persistent prediction errors, serving as the primary upstream
driver of epistemic anxiety. * \textbf{Noise:} The expectation of
irreducible, aleatoric uncertainty. This corresponds to the ambient
statistical ``floor'' of noise the agent expects to tolerate. High
expected noise represents an epistemic humility that prevents premature
cognitive closure, whereas a low noise expectation triggers drastic,
often maladaptive model updates when residual variance cannot be
eliminated.

\subsubsection{The Allostatic
Imperative}\label{the-allostatic-imperative}

This constitutes the pragmatic, metabolic drive to maintain systemic
integrity. * \textbf{Energy:} The expectation of allostatic reserve.
Grounded in predictive processing accounts of the ``body-budget''
(\citeproc{ref-barrett2017theory}{Barrett, 2017}), this parameter
dictates whether the agent expects to operate at a metabolic surplus or
a chronic deficit. * \textbf{Tractability:} The expectation that active
inference can reliably reduce prediction error. Formally related to the
expected precision of proprioceptive and control signals, tractability
determines whether the agent perceives the environment as controllable.
A low tractability expectation shares close computational parallels with
learned helplessness (\citeproc{ref-seligman1972learned}{Seligman,
1972}), driving epistemic shielding and behavioural inhibition as
energetically efficient responses to expected futility.

\subsubsection{The Teleological
Imperative}\label{the-teleological-imperative}

This imperative governs goal-directed purposiveness and orientation
toward finitude. * \textbf{Reward:} The expectation concerning the value
of available outcomes. Formally corresponding to the log prior
probability over preferred outcomes (the \(\mathbf{C}\)-vector in active
inference), it anchors approach motivation by weighting expected free
energy toward intrinsically valuable states. * \textbf{Horizon (The
Terminal Expectation):} The expected temporal depth of the agent's
policy space (parameter \(T\)). Within the FEP, mortality salience
functions as a precision-weighting problem: confrontation with finitude
forces the agent to resolve the prediction error of its anticipated
systemic cessation (\citeproc{ref-greenberg1986terror}{Greenberg et al.,
1986}). This defines whether the agent aims for \emph{Transcendence}
(extending the Markov blanket across time through cultural or biological
legacy), \emph{Integration}, or \emph{Disengagement} (collapsing
temporal depth to avoid the massive free energy of an uncontrollable
future, which, in its extreme, functions as the active inference
analogue of Thanatos).

\subsection{Level 2: Affective States}\label{level-2-affective-states}

The second level represents the physiological and affective readouts of
Level 1 priors. Deviating from classical discrete emotion theories, the
Deep-Self Model aligns with the theory of constructed emotion
(\citeproc{ref-barrett2017theory}{Barrett, 2017}), treating affect as
the phenomenological readout of predictive success or failure.

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Epistemic Arousal:} The felt interoceptive consequence of
  unresolved, high-precision prediction errors. Driven by high
  Volatility, chronic epistemic arousal forces the system to retreat to
  its most reliable data stream---the physical body---resulting in
  hyper-vigilance.
\item
  \textbf{Vitality:} The somatic readout of the body-budget. A high
  Vitality state signals surplus allostatic resources, facilitating
  structurally demanding behaviours like social coupling and
  exploration. Conversely, low Vitality reflects allostatic load.
\item
  \textbf{Valence:} The affective readout tracking the system's own Free
  Energy derivative. Modulated directly by Reward Expectation and
  constrained by Vitality, it represents anticipatory positive affect
  and the baseline Expected Free Energy tone of the system.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Level 3: Precision Biases}\label{level-3-precision-biases}

Affective states cascade downward to tune Level 3 Precision Biases. This
level forms the structural architecture of the self, mapping cleanly
onto phenomenological taxonomies of selfhood.

\subsubsection{The Minimal Self}\label{the-minimal-self}

Anchoring the physiological boundary of the Markov blanket, governed
heavily by interoceptive circuitry
(\citeproc{ref-seth2013interoceptive}{Seth, 2013}). *
\textbf{Interoception-Exteroception Bias:} Dictates reliance on internal
somatic signals versus external structure. *
\textbf{Differentiation-Dissolution Bias:} Governs the permeability of
the Markov blanket. \emph{Differentiation} favours autonomy and rigid
boundaries, motivating dominance-seeking to subjugate the environment
rather than revise the internal model. \emph{Dissolution} favours
structural synchrony, driving affiliation and the distribution of
cognitive load across the social group.

\subsubsection{The Agentic Self}\label{the-agentic-self}

Representing the cybernetic strategy for resolving expected free energy.
* \textbf{Action-Perception Bias:} The relative precision placed on
active inference (modifying the world) versus perceptual inference
(modifying internal beliefs). * \textbf{Exploration-Exploitation Bias:}
The precision assigned to epistemic foraging. Crucially, this drive is
split into two distinct operational domains: \textbf{Somatic Foraging}
(testing the physical Markov blanket through biological edge-seeking and
physical risk) and \textbf{Semantic Foraging} (testing the conceptual
boundaries of the generative model by probing worldview coherence
through transgressive art or profound altered states of consciousness).

\subsubsection{The Narrative Self}\label{the-narrative-self}

Governing diachronic (temporal) continuity. * \textbf{Rigidity-Fluidity
Bias:} The precision assigned to autobiographical stability. High
rigidity conserves identity defensively across contexts; fluidity allows
for rapid self-updating but introduces the risk of narrative
fragmentation (identity diffusion). * \textbf{Future-Present Bias:} A
temporal discounting bias determining how quickly the precision of
predicted outcomes degrades over time.

\subsection{Level 4: Phenotypic Attractors
(Observables)}\label{level-4-phenotypic-attractors-observables}

The confluence of Level 3 precision biases creates the stable attractor
basins that traditional psychometrics classify as traits. By
parameterising these deep computational layers, the Cascade Model
natively generates the phenotypic profiles spanning several major
psychological frameworks.

Within the \textbf{Five-Factor space}, \emph{Neuroticism} emerges not as
a fundamental trait, but as an attractor generated by chronically
elevated epistemic arousal feeding a heavy Interoception bias.
\emph{Extraversion} manifests through converging precision on the Action
pole and the Dissolution pole. \emph{Openness} requires a highly fluid
Narrative Self paired with an Exploration bias.

Similarly, the dimensions of \textbf{Attachment Theory} reflect the
Differentiation-Dissolution bias constrained by Level 1 priors.
\emph{Anxious attachment} emerges from high Epistemic Arousal driving
hyper-coupling (Dissolution), where the agent urgently uses another's
Markov blanket for co-regulation but lacks the tractability to trust the
relationship. \emph{Avoidant attachment} is driven by low Tractability
or high metabolic cost, prompting defensive Differentiation and
withdrawal.

\section{Relationship to Deep Structures and
Interventions}\label{relationship-to-deep-structures-and-interventions}

\subsubsection{Jungian Archetypes \& The REBUS
Model}\label{jungian-archetypes-the-rebus-model}

The topographic nature of the cascade accommodates deep phylogenetic
structures. Jungian archetypes can be formalised as deeply entrenched,
phylogenetically conserved structural expectations acting as ``strange
attractors'' within the free energy landscape
(\citeproc{ref-becker2019archetypes}{Becker \& Neuberg, 2019}).
Furthermore, the model aligns smoothly with the REBUS (Relaxed Beliefs
Under Psychedelics) framework
(\citeproc{ref-carhart2019rebus}{Carhart-Harris \& Friston, 2019}). The
therapeutic benefits of extreme semantic foraging via psychedelics can
be understood as the functional consequence of transient attractor-wall
shallowing---liberating the agent from pathologically stable
configurations and allowing the system to settle into adjacent basins
with lower expected free energy.

\section{Limitations and Open
Questions}\label{limitations-and-open-questions}

The most pressing empirical challenge for the Deep-Self Model is
developing valid measurement instruments capable of assessing the
proposed expectation and bias parameters. Traditional subjective
questionnaires predominantly capture the downstream ``observables'' of
personality; they do not directly access the unconscious precision
weightings that form the actual predictive scaffolding of experience.

Because these deeply entrenched generative parameters operate outside
declarative awareness, implicit behavioural and psychophysical tasks
offer a much more promising path forward. Paradigms investigating
illusion sensitivity and phenomenological control are particularly
well-suited to probe the underlying predictive architecture directly.
Future research should leverage machine psychophysics and computational
modelling to reliably back-estimate these deep, sub-personal parameters
from task performance, providing rigorous empirical validation for the
cascade hierarchy.
